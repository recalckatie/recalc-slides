Connectors, Skills, and Memory are very powerful ideas for building an agent. And they are remarkably easy to set up.

Managing AI Risk

Before You Connect

Manage Risk

AI can make mistakes or act differently than you would.
Bad actors can also trick AI through hidden instructions in content it reads (prompt injection).

Monitor closely

Watch what Claude is doing closely. It will explicitly ask for permissions from you. And if you see it doing something you don’t like, hit “stop” or escape.

Start conservative

Make more conservative choices to start (e.g. “put this in drafts” rather than directly sending an email without your review).

Be Transparent

Consider having it add “sent with Claude” to the bottom of messages.

Sandbox

Consider modeling after a real executive assistant to sandbox access. Katie created a new email for her AI assistant with access to a limited set of folders, its own inbox, and delegate calendar access. Much like you’d set up with a human EA.

AI Chat — Key Difference Makers

AI Chat

AI Chat: Get Better Results

Three things that make a big difference

Use a thinking model

  • Select “Thinking” mode instead of quick response for most work
  • ChatGPT: GPT-5.2 Thinking (dropdown at upper left)
  • Claude: Opus 4.6

Ask AI to write your prompt

  • For complex requests, ask AI to draft the prompt for you first
  • Provide example inputs/outputs for more specificity

Treat it like a junior employee

  • Give context — background and why the work matters
  • Give clear instructions, then check its work
  • Question it if something feels off
